Coordination of Benefits (COB)

When a patient has two dental insurance plans, understanding which plan pays first and how much the second plan pays is one of the most important billing skills you will use in practice.

What is Coordination of Benefits?

Coordination of Benefits (COB) is the process used when a patient has coverage under two or more dental insurance plans. COB rules determine which plan pays first (primary) and which pays second (secondary). The goal is to prevent the patient from receiving more in benefits than the actual cost of treatment.

The Good News for Patients

When COB is handled correctly, patients with two insurance plans often have little or no out-of-pocket cost. Always verify both plans before treatment so you can give the patient an accurate estimate.

The Billing Reality

COB errors are one of the most common causes of claim denials in dental practice. Filing claims in the wrong order, forgetting to send the primary EOB to the secondary, or not knowing which plan is primary can result in significant delays and patient frustration.

How to Determine Which Plan is Primary

Rule 1 — Employee vs Dependent

The plan in which the patient is enrolled as the employee or main policyholder is always primary. The plan in which the patient is enrolled as a dependent is secondary.

Example: Maria is covered under her own employer plan AND as a dependent on her spouse plan. Maria own employer plan is always primary.

Rule 2 — The Birthday Rule (for dependent children)

When a child is covered under both parents plans, the plan of the parent whose birthday falls earlier in the calendar year is primary. Based on month and day only — not year of birth. Age does not matter.

ParentBirthdayStatus
Parent AMarch 26PRIMARY
Parent BJune 11SECONDARY

Parent A plan is primary because March comes before June — even if Parent B is older.

Rule 3 — Same Birthday Exception

If both parents share the same birthday, the plan that has covered the parent for the longest time is primary.

Rule 4 — Divorced or Separated Parents

The court decree takes precedence over the birthday rule. If the decree gives joint custody without mentioning healthcare, the birthday rule applies.

Always ask about divorce decrees for minor patients — they override the birthday rule.

Rule 5 — Active Employment vs COBRA or Retiree Plan

The active employer plan is always primary over a COBRA or retiree plan.

Rule 6 — Two Employer Plans

The plan that has covered the patient the longest is primary.

Rule 7 — Medical Plan with Dental Benefits + Separate Dental Plan

The medical plan is typically primary.

CRITICAL — Medicaid (Denti-Cal) is ALWAYS Last

Denti-Cal / Medicaid is Always the Payer of Last Resort

If a patient has both Denti-Cal AND private insurance, you must bill the private insurance first. Denti-Cal is always secondary. Failing to bill private insurance before Denti-Cal is a compliance violation and the claim will be denied.

Common Mistake at Dental Schools

Many students see a Denti-Cal card and bill only Denti-Cal without asking if the patient has other insurance. Always ask every patient: "Do you have any other dental or medical insurance besides what you showed me today?"

Types of Coordination of Benefits

Not all secondary plans coordinate the same way. Which method applies depends on the patient specific secondary plan.

Traditional COB — Best for Patient

The secondary plan pays after the primary, potentially bringing total reimbursement up to 100% of the procedure cost.

$1,000 procedure. Primary pays $800. Secondary pays up to $200. Patient owes $0.

Carve-Out COB — Moderate

The secondary calculates what it would have paid if primary, then subtracts what the primary already paid.

$1,000 procedure. Secondary would pay $750 if primary. Primary paid $800. Secondary pays $0.

Non-Duplication COB — Worst for Patient

If the primary paid the same or more than the secondary would have paid, the secondary pays nothing. Common in self-funded plans. California has banned this for fully insured plans.

$1,000 procedure. Primary pays $800. Secondary would have paid $750. Secondary pays $0 because $800 is more than $750.

How to Know Which Type Applies

Call the secondary carrier and ask: "Does your plan use traditional, carve-out, or non-duplication coordination of benefits?" Document the answer in the patient chart before telling the patient their out-of-pocket cost.

Step-by-Step COB Claims Process
1

Verify both insurance plans at the patient first visit

Ask every patient: "Do you have any other dental or medical insurance?" Get both insurance cards, call both carriers to verify eligibility and coverage, and determine which plan is primary.

2

Submit a pre-treatment estimate to both plans

Before major treatment, submit a pre-treatment estimate to the primary. Once you receive the primary response, submit it to the secondary as well so you can give the patient an accurate estimate.

3

Submit the claim to the primary plan first

Submit the claim to the primary insurance. Wait for the primary Explanation of Benefits (EOB) before submitting to the secondary. Never submit both claims simultaneously.

4

Receive and keep the primary EOB

The primary plan sends an EOB showing what was billed, what was allowed, what was paid, and what the patient owes. You must send this to the secondary plan.

5

Submit to the secondary plan with the primary EOB attached

Submit a new claim to the secondary plan with a copy of the primary EOB attached. Most secondary plans will not process without it.

6

Bill the patient for any remaining balance

After both plans have paid, bill the patient for any remaining balance. Clearly explain what each plan paid and what the patient responsibility is.

Common COB Mistakes Dental Students Make
❌
Not asking about secondary insurance. Always ask every patient if they have additional coverage — people frequently forget to mention a second plan.
❌
Filing both claims at the same time. The secondary requires the primary EOB before processing. Filing simultaneously causes the secondary to deny.
❌
Getting primary and secondary backwards. Filing to the wrong plan first causes both claims to be denied or delayed.
❌
Billing Denti-Cal before private insurance. Private insurance is ALWAYS primary when a patient has both. Filing to Denti-Cal first is a compliance violation.
❌
Forgetting to attach the primary EOB to the secondary claim. The secondary will automatically deny without it.
❌
Assuming the secondary covers the full remaining balance. Depending on the COB method — especially non-duplication — the secondary may pay nothing.
❌
Not verifying COB type before treatment. Always ask the secondary carrier which COB method they use before telling the patient their out-of-pocket cost.
